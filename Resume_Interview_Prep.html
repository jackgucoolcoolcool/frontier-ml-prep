§1 — Multimodal data sourcing strategy

Résumé: "Create MM data sourcing strategy on large-scale webpages, worlds of PDFs, books, files, human-generated agentic traces, acquired sources, and more."

Mock — what "I" did I owned the multimodal pretraining data strategy: deciding which sources to invest in, how to turn them into trainable image-text (and interleaved) examples, and how to balance the mixture. Concretely I (a) built pipelines that render and pair web pages, PDFs, books, and files into interleaved image+text with alt-text/layout/structure preserved; (b) prioritized document and agentic-trace sources because they teach grounding (OCR, layout, UI actions) that captions don't; (c) ran data-mixture ablations to set source weights against downstream capability evals; and (d) negotiated acquired/licensed corpora where the open web was thin (high-quality docs, specialized verticals). The north star was capability-per-token: every source justified by the eval it moves.
implementation How do you turn a webpage/PDF into a multimodal training example?▶
Render the document to images at native resolution and also extract its structure: DOM/HTML for pages, the text layer + layout boxes for PDFs (with OCR fallback for scanned pages). Produce several training views from one source: (1) interleaved image+text following reading order (image regions inline with their text), (2) derendering pairs (image → HTML/markdown/table), (3) OCR/grounding pairs (image → text with bounding boxes). Preserve alt-text and figure captions as natural image-text pairs. Pipeline concerns: dedup near-identical pages, filter boilerplate/NSFW/PII, normalize resolution and aspect ratio (native-res tiling, Day 4 §1.5), and tag each example with source/quality metadata for mixture control.
problems & future What's hard about agentic traces and acquired data?▶
Agentic traces (human UI sessions, tool-use logs) are gold for grounding actions to screen state, but they're noisy (mis-clicks, dead time), privacy-sensitive (must scrub PII/credentials), and distributionally narrow (a few apps dominate). Future: synthesize/augment traces, learn from successful-outcome filtering, and close the loop with the agent generating its own traces (self-improvement). Acquired data raises licensing, provenance, and contamination risks (could overlap eval sets), and you must verify quality before paying for scale. The frontier problem: the open web is being exhausted for high-quality tokens, so document understanding, synthetic data, and licensed verticals are where marginal capability now comes from.
nuggets & taste What's the non-obvious insight about MM data?▶
For multimodal, perception is data-bound before it's model-bound: OCR/doc/chart/UI capability tracks how much structured, grounded data you have far more than raw caption volume — which is why document and derendering data punch above their weight (it gives dense, verifiable supervision, unlike vague captions). And the right unit isn't "tokens" but capability-per-token: I'd justify every source by a compute-matched mixture ablation against the specific eval it should move, not by size. This ties to the "Science of Data" and scaling work below.

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep How do you build a derendering pretraining corpus at scale, and what's the failure mode?▶
Render millions of (source → image) pairs programmatically: HTML/CSS pages, matplotlib/Vega/Plotly charts, LaTeX equations/tables, spreadsheets — exact paired supervision, essentially free. This is the Pix2Struct recipe (screenshot→structured-markup as the pretraining pretext, masked-screenshot parsing to a simplified HTML), extended by MatCha (chart derendering + math-reasoning pretraining on top of Pix2Struct, ~+20% on ChartQA/PlotQA) and DePlot (image→data-table, then let an LLM reason). Failure mode = the synthetic↔real distribution gap: programmatic renders are too clean (fonts, anti-aliasing, no scan noise, tidy layouts), so models overfit synthetic and fail on real scanned PDFs/screenshots. Fixes: heavy render diversity + degradations (blur, JPEG, skew, backgrounds), mix in mined real pairs, and always validate on real benchmarks (ChartQA/DocVQA), never just held-out synthetic. The payoff: re-rendering gives a verifiable reward (§5).
deep Interleaved image–text data: how do you construct it, and do you put loss on the image tokens?▶
Follow document reading order, inserting visual tokens inline where each figure/region appears (the MMC4 / OBELICS open-web recipe), so the text that references an image is adjacent to it and self/cross-attention can bind them. Key training decision: for an understanding model you typically mask the LM loss on visual tokens (predict text only); for a unified generation model you include image-token loss (predict image tokens too). You also weight each source in the mixture and cap document length. Getting co-location and ordering right is what makes grounding (which text refers to which figure) learnable rather than a bag of images.
deep How do you keep web/PDF/acquired sources from contaminating perception evals?▶
The open web contains the very benchmarks you measure (ChartQA images, DocVQA pages, MMMU items). Defenses: image-hash + embedding-kNN dedup of training data against eval images, n-gram overlap on OCR-extracted text, canary checks, and a preference for time-gated / freshly-collected / private evals. For acquired corpora, audit provenance and overlap before paying for scale. Contamination inflates exactly the perception metrics you're trying to move, so this is non-negotiable for honest capability tracking (Day 3).

§2 — Science of Data

Résumé: "Co-develop MM data embedding, clustering, visualization, searching, repeats/dedups, filtering, training-data attribution, and data self-improving."

Mock — what "I" did I helped build the tooling and methodology to treat data as an experimental object: embed every example, cluster and visualize the corpus to see what's actually in it, search it semantically, detect near-duplicates (MinHash/LSH + embedding kNN), filter by quality/safety classifiers, attribute model behaviors back to training examples, and run self-improvement loops where the model helps clean/expand its own data. The point is to make data decisions measurable rather than folkloric.
implementation How do you dedup and embed a web-scale multimodal corpus?▶
Dedup: exact (hashing) + fuzzy (MinHash signatures → LSH banding → candidate pairs → verify Jaccard; Day 3) on text, plus embedding kNN (ANN over image/text embeddings) to catch semantic near-dups and image re-uploads. Embedding: encode each example with a strong frozen encoder (CLIP/SigLIP for image-text, a text embedder for text), index with ANN (IVF-PQ / HNSW) for billions of items. Use the embeddings for everything else: cluster (k-means / hierarchical) to map the corpus, visualize (UMAP) to eyeball coverage/gaps, semantic search to audit, and quality/safety filtering via lightweight classifiers on the embeddings. Engineering: this is a distributed-systems problem (sharded indices, streaming) as much as an ML one.
problems & future What is training-data attribution, and why is it hard at scale?▶
TDA = which training examples caused a given behavior/output? Classic tools: influence functions (approximate the effect of up-weighting an example via the inverse-Hessian-vector product), and scalable estimators like TRAK / datamodels (linearize the model, use random projections). Hard because: the Hessian is intractable for billion-param models (need approximations), influence is noisy/non-additive in deep nets, and attribution at the scale of trillions of tokens is a massive retrieval problem. Why it matters: debugging (why did it memorize this?), contamination/copyright tracing, and data curation (drop harmful-influence data, up-weight helpful). Future: cheaper, more faithful attribution and using it to steer the data mixture automatically.
nuggets & taste What's the research-taste view of "data self-improving"?▶
The lever is verifiability + filtering: a model can improve its own data when there's a way to check the synthetic example is good — rejection-sampling correct solutions (math/code), self-critiquing and keeping only validated outputs, or distilling a strong model's behavior. The trap is model collapse / echo chambers: train on unfiltered self-generated data and you amplify your own biases and lose tail diversity. So the taste is "self-improvement is real exactly to the extent you have an external check," which is the same insight as RLVR (Day 6) and the BLIP CapFilt lineage (Day 4). I'd always pair generation with a filter and monitor distributional drift.

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep Training-data attribution at LLM scale — how do you make influence tractable, and what surprised the field?▶
The exact influence function needs an inverse-Hessian–vector product over billions of params — intractable. Practical estimators: EK-FAC (Eigenvalue-corrected Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature — block-diagonal, layer-wise Kronecker factors; the method behind Anthropic's "Studying LLM Generalization with Influence Functions"), TRAK (linearize the trained model, random-project per-example gradients down to ~tens-of-thousands of dims, ensemble over checkpoints), and TracIn (sum gradient dot-products along the training trajectory). Random projection alone cuts an ~8-billion-dim gradient to under 100k dims — an ~80,000× memory/retrieval reduction. The surprising finding: the examples that most influence a model's knowledge of a fact are often not the ones that state the fact — influence is abstract/procedural, which reshapes how you curate (and reason about copyright/memorization).
deep DoReMi vs Data-Mixing-Laws for domain weights — and why don't the weights transfer?▶
DoReMi: train a small proxy with Group DRO to find domain weights minimizing worst-case excess loss across domains, then train the big model on the reweighted mix (280M proxy → 8B target: +6.5% avg few-shot, 2.6× fewer steps to baseline). Data Mixing Laws: fit a predictive function of loss vs mixture from small runs and optimize the mix analytically, nested with size/step scaling laws — no DRO loop. The catch: optimal weights are entangled with tokenizer / architecture / scale, so proxy weights don't transfer cleanly across model families — you re-fit per setup. Taste: the mixture is a learned hyperparameter validated against downstream evals, not a constant.
deep Model collapse — the mechanism, and how do you self-improve safely?▶
Recursive training on your own outputs causes the "curse of recursion": sampling favors high-probability outputs, so variance shrinks, tails (rare modes) vanish, and biases amplify; finite-sample error compounds geometrically. Mitigations with theory/evidence: (1) accumulate real + synthetic rather than replacing real data (replacement provably grows test error; accumulation bounds it); (2) verify/filter synthetic — self-verification or an external checker provably prevents collapse (the RLVR / rejection-sampling-fine-tuning / STaR lineage works because there's a correctness filter); (3) keep a real-data anchor and monitor diversity. One-liner: "self-improvement is safe exactly to the degree you have an external check."

§3 — Scaling: hero compute, laddering, single-change ablation

Résumé: "Co-develop scaling law for optimal hero compute, laddering, and MM single-change ablation."

Mock — what "I" did I worked on the scaling methodology for the multimodal stack: fitting scaling laws so the next hero run (the single largest, most expensive training run) spends its compute on the right $N$/$D$/resolution allocation; building a ladder of small→large models so hyperparameters and recipe choices transfer upward predictably; and enforcing single-change ablations so every recipe decision is attributable. The goal: never gamble the hero run — predict it.

Laddering: fit a scaling law on cheap small runs, extrapolate to choose and de-risk the hero run's allocation — and validate by predicting an intermediate run before committing.

implementation How do you set the hero run's $N$/$D$/resolution, and what is "laddering"?▶
Fit a scaling law (Chinchilla-style $L(N,D)=E+A/N^\alpha+B/D^\beta$, or IsoFLOP profiles; Day 5/Day 4 §1.9) on a ladder of small runs; solve the constrained minimization for $N_\text{opt},D_\text{opt}$ at the hero budget. For multimodal, add axes the LLM law doesn't have — image resolution / #visual tokens, the vision-encoder size, and the data mixture — and fit the optimal allocation across modalities (MM1's finding: resolution + image tokens dominate). Laddering = a staircase of model sizes where you transfer hyperparameters predictably — e.g. μP (maximal-update parameterization) so learning rate/init transfer from small to large — so the hero run inherits a tuned recipe instead of being tuned from scratch. Validate the law by predicting a held-out mid-scale run.
problems & future Why are multimodal scaling laws harder than text-only?▶
Extra confounded axes (resolution, encoder size, modality mixture, fusion choice) make the "one compute axis" assumption break; the optimal allocation shifts with task type (perception-bound vs language-bound). The dependent variable is messy — pretraining loss is smooth but downstream capability (OCR, grounding) is what you care about and can be noisier/emergent. And the encoder-vs-LLM compute split is itself a scaling decision (encoder-heavy vs early-fusion; Apple's NMM study). Future: laws that jointly predict the compute split and resolution, and that hold across the understanding↔generation unification.
nuggets & taste Why insist on "single-change" ablations at scale?▶
Because at frontier scale, confounds are everywhere and runs are too expensive to repeat — if you change three things and loss moves, you've learned nothing attributable. A disciplined one-variable, compute-matched ablation is the only way to build a recipe you can trust and extrapolate. The taste point: I'd rather have ten clean single-change deltas than one heroic run with five simultaneous changes — the former composes into a predictive recipe; the latter is folklore. This is the experimental-design habit from Day 5 applied where it's most expensive to get wrong.

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep μP / μTransfer — what exactly transfers across scale, and what still needs care?▶
μP (maximal update parameterization) re-scales init and per-layer learning rates so that, in the infinite-width limit, (i) preactivations are $O(1)$ at init, (ii) the network output is $O(1)$ during training, and (iii) features update at $O(1)$ — "maximal feature learning" at every width. Consequence: the optimal LR (and several HPs) become width-invariant, so you tune on a small model and zero-shot transfer to the large one (μTransfer; Cerebras-GPT shipped this). What still needs care: transfer is primarily across width (and partly depth) — batch size, data mixture, and total-token schedule still interact with scale, so you validate that the small-model optimum actually predicts the large-model loss curve before trusting it.
deep How do you de-risk a hero run before spending the compute?▶
Build a ladder of small compute-matched runs; fit both an IsoFLOP profile and a parametric $L(N,D)=E+A/N^\alpha+B/D^\beta$; then validate by predicting a held-out intermediate run and checking it lands within error before extrapolating to the hero budget. Combine with μTransfer'd hyperparameters so the hero inherits a tuned recipe rather than being tuned blind. For multimodal, add resolution / encoder-size / mixture axes and fit the compute split. The rule: never let an untested change ride into the hero run — predict, confirm, then commit.

§4 — Multimodal pretraining (all Gemini versions)

Résumé: "Core contributor to Gemini from 1.0, focus on multimodal pretraining."

Mock — what "I" did I contributed to the multimodal pretraining recipe: how images/video/audio enter the model (fusion), how the perception stack is sized and scaled, the training objectives and data mixture, and the capability evals that gate progress. Practically that's choosing native-resolution handling, the encoder/early-fusion strategy, the interleaved-data format, and running the ablations that decide each.
implementation Walk through the architecture choices in a frontier multimodal model.▶
The spectrum (Day 4 §1.4): cross-attention (Flamingo — frozen LLM, gated x-attn), concatenation/projector (LLaVA — project visual tokens, prepend), or early fusion (Chameleon/native — one transformer on interleaved image+text tokens). Frontier "omni" models trend toward native early fusion with strong perception: native-resolution handling (dynamic tiling, M-RoPE for 2-D+temporal position), token compression (Perceiver/Q-Former/pixel-shuffle) to keep sequence length sane, and a vision stack sized by the scaling work. Objectives: next-token prediction over interleaved multimodal sequences, often plus contrastive/captioning auxiliaries. The decisions are made by compute-matched ablations against perception + reasoning evals.
problems & future Encoder-heavy vs native early-fusion — where's it going?▶
Today, encoder-heavy two-tower (reuse a strong pretrained vision encoder, scale resolution) wins on perception now by reusing contrastive/self-supervised features; native early-fusion is more compute-efficient at scale and the likely endpoint (Apple's NMM scaling study). The tension is real and it's a live research question I'd frame explicitly. The convergence point is unified omni models that ingest and generate all modalities (Day 4 §1.8), where the encoder/LLM boundary dissolves — but getting OCR/doc/3D perception to match the two-tower stack from scratch is the open challenge.
nuggets & taste What's the highest-leverage lever in MM pretraining?▶
Resolution and visual-token budget, and the data mixture — MM1 found these dominate the connector/architecture details. The non-obvious part: a lot of "model" problems in multimodal are actually perception problems solved by letting the model see more pixels (and giving it grounded data), not by a bigger LLM. So I'd spend marginal compute on the encoder/resolution and on document/grounding data before scaling the language tower — exactly the "encoder-heavy" allocation argument, justified by a scaling-law ablation.

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep Native dynamic resolution + M-RoPE — implementation details and what breaks.▶
Naive dynamic resolution (Qwen2-VL): process arbitrary aspect ratios into a variable number of patch tokens with no padding/squashing, so a detailed image yields more tokens (preserving info downsampling would destroy). To encode position without a fixed grid, M-RoPE decomposes the rotary embedding into 1-D (text), 2-D (image height/width), and 3-D (video time) components. What breaks: variable token counts complicate batching/packing and cost (a high-res page → thousands of tokens → sequence-length blowup), so you pair it with token compression + resolution caps; and positional generalization to unseen resolutions needs validation. SigLIP 2's NaFlex variant is the contrastive-encoder analog (native aspect ratio + variable resolution).
deep Token compression — Perceiver Resampler vs Q-Former vs pixel-shuffle.▶
All map many patch tokens → a small fixed set, decoupling "what the encoder sees" from "what the LLM pays for." Perceiver Resampler / Q-Former = learned-query cross-attention (attentional pooling) → flexible output count, but a learned bottleneck that can drop fine text (hurts OCR). Pixel-shuffle / pooling = merge adjacent patches (e.g. 2×2→1) → cheap, preserves locality, used in LLaVA-NeXT / InternVL. Tradeoff: aggressive compression kills OCR/dense tasks, so for perception-heavy work you keep more tokens (encoder-heavy) and compress less — exactly the §4 allocation argument.

§5 — Capabilities: derendering, OCR, 2D spatial, 3D physical intelligence

Résumé: "Define, strategize, and execute key capabilities: derendering, OCR, 2D spatial, and 3D physical intelligence."

Mock — what "I" did I defined and drove a set of grounded perception capabilities as first-class objectives — not emergent hopes but explicitly built and measured: derendering (image → structured source: chart→table, screenshot→code/DOM, doc→markup), OCR (robust multilingual, layout-aware text reading), 2D spatial (detection, pointing, grounding, referring expressions), and 3D physical intelligence (depth, 3-D layout, physics/affordances for embodied use). For each I owned the data strategy, the training objective, and the eval that gates it.
implementation How do you train derendering, and why is it valuable?▶
Derendering = inverse rendering: map a rendered image back to the program/structure that produced it (chart→data table or plotting code, screenshot→HTML/DOM, equation image→LaTeX, document→markup). Train on synthetic pairs you can generate at scale — render millions of (source, image) pairs from real charts/HTML/LaTeX — plus mined real pairs. It's uniquely valuable because the supervision is verifiable: you can re-render the model's predicted source and compare to the input image (a programmatic reward), which makes it ideal for RLVR-style refinement and self-improvement. Lineage: Pix2Struct / MatCha / DePlot. It also forces genuine grounding — you can't hallucinate a chart's numbers if you must reproduce it.
implementation How is 2D spatial grounding represented and supervised?▶
Express boxes/points as tokens — quantize coordinates into the vocabulary (e.g. <x=0.42><y=0.71> or special location tokens) so detection/pointing/referring becomes sequence prediction the LLM head handles natively (Pix2Seq / Qwen-VL grounding / Molmo pointing). Supervise with detection/grounding datasets and synthetic layouts; evaluate with localization metrics (IoU, pointing accuracy) and referring-expression accuracy. The key implementation choice is coordinate tokenization granularity (resolution of the grid) and making it robust to native/any resolution (M-RoPE).
problems & future What makes "3D physical intelligence" hard from 2D-trained models?▶
2D web data has weak 3D/physics supervision — captions rarely specify depth, contact, support, or dynamics. Models learn appearance priors, not a world model, so they fail on occlusion reasoning, metric depth, stability/physics, and affordances. Hard parts: scarce 3D-grounded data (need depth/LiDAR/sim), evaluating mechanism not just plausible answers (Day 5 Drill 14), and bridging to action (VLA, robotics — your Waymo-advisor and embodied threads). Future: more simulation + sensor data, video for temporal/physical dynamics, and unifying perception with prediction/action so the model's 3D understanding is checked by whether its predicted motions are physically consistent.
nuggets & taste What ties OCR/derender/2D/3D together as a strategy?▶
They're all grounding — forcing the model's outputs to be checkable against the pixels rather than fluent guesses — and they're ordered by how verifiable the supervision is (OCR and derendering are the most verifiable → highest-ROI, can self-improve via re-rendering; 3D/physics the least → the frontier). The taste move is to invest where you get verifiable, dense supervision first (it compounds via self-improvement and RLVR) and to treat fluent-but-ungrounded captioning as the enemy. This is the same "verifiability" thread as RLVR (Day 6) and data self-improvement (§2).

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep Closing the re-render loop for derendering RL — how, and the gotchas.▶
The reward is programmatic: execute the model's predicted source (run the plotting code / render the HTML/LaTeX) and compare to the input — but compare on a canonical representation (the parsed data table à la DePlot, or the DOM) rather than raw pixels, because rendering is nondeterministic (fonts, backends, anti-aliasing). Use the signal for rejection sampling / RLVR. Gotchas: (1) partial credit — a chart with one wrong bar needs a graded reward, not 0/1; (2) reward hacking — output that renders similar but is structurally wrong, so check structure/values, not appearance; (3) executor sandboxing and latency. This is OCR/derendering's superpower: verifiable supervision other generation tasks lack.
deep Coordinate tokenization for 2-D grounding — the design choices.▶
Represent boxes/points as tokens — either quantize coordinates into $N$ bins as special location tokens (Pix2Seq) or emit normalized numeric coordinates as text (Qwen-VL). Choices that matter: normalize to $[0,1]$ vs absolute pixels (normalize for any-resolution robustness); grid granularity (too coarse → imprecise; too fine → vocab/length blowup); ordering of multiple objects (raster/confidence order, or a set-prediction loss); pointing (Molmo) vs boxes; and binding the referring expression text to its coordinates. Evaluate with IoU / pointing accuracy and language correctness — a box can be right while the label is wrong.
deep VLA action representation — discrete tokens vs flow-matching, and the eval problem.▶
RT-2 tokenizes robot actions as discrete text tokens so a VLM (PaLI-X / PaLM-E backbone, co-trained on web + robot data) emits them autoregressively — inheriting semantic generalization and even CoT ("pick something to clean a spill" → sponge). π0 instead uses a flow-matching action head for continuous, smooth, high-frequency trajectories (better for dexterity), trained on 10k+ hours. OpenVLA (7B) adapts to a new task with ~50–200 demos. The eval problem: real-robot success is expensive/unsafe and sim-to-real gaps mislead — you need careful task suites and remember "looks right" ≠ "physically succeeds." It's the grounding/verifiability theme pushed to the action level — and the bridge to 3-D physical intelligence.

§6 — MMU4MMGen & MMGen4MMU (understanding ↔ generation)

Résumé: "MMU4MMGen — understanding improves generation via self-critiquing & grounded coding-sketching (Omni-flash). MMGen4MMU — generation improves understanding via visual thinking & dense prediction (Vision Banana)."

Mock — what "I" did I co-led the understanding↔generation flywheel: using a strong understanding model to make generation better (it critiques and grounds the generator's outputs, and "sketches" via code), and using generation to make understanding better (the model "thinks visually" — draws/imagines intermediate images — and learns dense prediction by having to render). The thesis: understanding and generation are two views of one world model, and each is the other's best teacher.
implementation How does understanding improve generation (MMU4MMGen)?▶
Three mechanisms: (1) self-critiquing / verification — the understanding model scores or critiques generated images (does it match the prompt? is the text legible? are objects/counts right?), giving a reward or a rejection-sampling filter that lifts generation quality (a multimodal RLAIF/best-of-n; Day 6). (2) grounded coding-sketching — instead of painting pixels directly, generate a program (HTML/SVG/code) that renders the target, which the understanding model can verify by re-reading the render — exact, editable, and verifiable (ties to derendering, §5). (3) better captions/labels for the generator's training data. The flywheel: understanding provides the checkable signal generation lacks.
implementation How does generation improve understanding (MMGen4MMU, "visual thinking")?▶
(1) Visual chain-of-thought / "thinking with images" — the model generates or edits intermediate images (crop, annotate, draw, imagine an unseen view) as reasoning steps, which helps on spatial/physical tasks a text CoT can't express. (2) Dense-prediction pretext — making the model generate depth maps, segmentations, or future frames forces a richer world model than discriminative labels alone (generation as a representation-learning objective; cf. the MMGen4MMU / unified-model line and "Nano Banana"-style image generation feeding back into perception). (3) Synthetic data — the generator creates controlled training/eval scenes (counterfactual physics, rare layouts) for understanding. The thesis: predicting pixels is a harder, denser objective than predicting labels, so it teaches more.
problems & future What are the risks of the understanding↔generation loop?▶
Reward/critic hacking (the generator games the understanding model's blind spots), distribution collapse (self-generated data narrows diversity — the §2 trap), and error accumulation (a weak critic teaches wrong lessons). Mitigations are the same verifiability discipline: prefer programmatic checks (re-render, run code, exact-match) over learned critics where possible, keep human/real-data anchors, and monitor diversity. Future: a single unified omni model where understanding and generation share weights so the flywheel is internal (Chameleon/Janus/Transfusion lineage, Day 4 §1.8) — the question is whether unified models can match specialists on both sides.
nuggets & taste Why is "grounded coding-sketching" a high-taste idea?▶
Because it converts an unverifiable generation problem into a verifiable one: a program that renders to the target can be executed and checked (re-render and compare), so you get a programmatic reward, editability, and compositionality for free — exactly what pixel-space generation lacks. It's the multimodal analog of "make the reward verifiable" (RLVR) and connects derendering, code, and generation into one loop. The deeper bet is that understanding and generation are dual and a model that can both see and draw has a more consistent world model than either alone — the central thesis I'd defend.

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep Janus vs Chameleon vs Transfusion — the real architectural tradeoff in unified models.▶
The core tension: understanding wants high-semantic features (CLIP-like), generation wants reconstruction-detail features (VQ/pixel) — one shared visual encoder is suboptimal for both. Chameleon: one early-fusion transformer over VQ image+text tokens (shared space, can generate, but the discrete tokenizer caps fidelity and understanding). Janus: decouples the visual encoding into two pathways (a semantic encoder for understanding, a generation codebook for synthesis) feeding one transformer — resolving the conflict; Janus-Pro scales data+model. Transfusion: one transformer trains text with next-token and images with diffusion (continuous → higher fidelity than VQ-AR). Variants: Show-o (discrete diffusion), JanusFlow (flow matching). The axis to name: shared-vs-decoupled encoding × autoregressive-vs-diffusion generation.
deep Does understanding actually inform generation, and how would you measure it?▶
It's an open, debated question — recent analyses find unified models don't automatically transfer understanding into generation. Measure it causally: hold the generator fixed and vary the understanding signal (better captions / self-critique on vs off), then measure prompt faithfulness, compositionality, counting, spatial relations, and legible text rendering on controlled prompts — compute-matched. If understanding genuinely informs generation, improving the critic should lift exactly the attributes the critic can judge, and not others. That falsifiable prediction is the high-taste way to defend (or kill) the MMU4MMGen thesis.
deep Self-critique reward — how do you stop the generator from hacking the critic?▶
Prefer programmatic / verifiable checks over a learned critic wherever possible (re-render and compare, OCR the generated text for legibility, run code). When you must use a learned critic: ensemble it, refresh it on-policy (the generator drifts into its blind spots — an adversarial dynamic identical to reward hacking in RL, Day 6), keep human / real-data anchors, and monitor diversity to catch collapse. The deeper bet — understanding and generation are dual views of one world model — only pays off if the critic's signal is honest, which is why verifiability is the through-line.

§7 — Multimodal post-training (Gemini 1 & 3)

Résumé: "Post-training (Gemini 1 and Gemini 3)."

Mock — what "I" did I worked on multimodal post-training: SFT on instruction/grounding data, preference optimization (RLHF/DPO) for multimodal helpfulness and reduced hallucination, and verifiable-reward RL (RLVR) where the multimodal answer is checkable (OCR exact-match, derendering re-render, grounded counting). The aim: take a strong perceiver and make it a reliable, honest, instruction-following assistant that doesn't hallucinate what it can't see.
implementation How is multimodal post-training different from text-only?▶
Same pillars (SFT → preference → verifiable RL; Day 6) but with multimodal-specific signals: (1) grounding/anti-hallucination preferences — prefer answers supported by the image (penalize objects/text not present; POPE-style); (2) verifiable multimodal rewards — OCR exact-match, re-render-and-compare for derendering, IoU/pointing for grounding, program execution for chart QA; (3) data must pair the image with the response, and reward models must be multimodal. The big win is that perception tasks are often verifiable, so RLVR applies directly — unlike fuzzy text helpfulness.
problems & future What's the hardest multimodal post-training problem?▶
Hallucination under weak grounding: the language prior overrides the (often low-res) visual evidence, and preference/RLHF can worsen it (the model learns confident, fluent answers that humans rate highly even when ungrounded — reward hacking the rater). Fixes: grounded reward models, verifiable rewards where possible, higher resolution (a perception fix, not a post-training fix), and abstention training ("say you can't read it"). Future: process rewards over visual reasoning chains, and extending verifiable RL beyond the few checkable tasks to general visual reliability.
nuggets & taste Where does multimodal post-training have an unfair advantage?▶
Verifiability. A surprising amount of multimodal capability is checkable — OCR, derendering (re-render), counting, grounding (IoU), chart QA (run the code) — so you can do RLVR with ground-truth rewards that text "helpfulness" can't. The taste move is to route every capability through its most verifiable formulation and harvest that signal for self-improvement and RL, reserving expensive human preferences for the genuinely fuzzy parts. That's the same verifiability thread running through §2, §5, §6.

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep Why does text-only DPO fail for VLM hallucination, and what fixes it?▶
Standard DPO on (chosen, rejected) text teaches which answer is preferred but lets the model satisfy the preference using its language prior while ignoring the image — it can even learn to attend less to vision. Fixes that re-ground it: mDPO adds an image preference — construct a corrupted/irrelevant image as the negative so the model must prefer the response given the correct image (conditioning the preference on visual grounding). RLHF-V collects fine-grained segment-level human corrections and applies dense DPO (DDPO) on the corrected spans. POVID / V-DPO synthesize hallucinated negatives (noise the image / inject hallucination). On-policy preference data matters (off-policy DPO drifts). Evaluate with POPE (object-presence) and hallucination benchmarks.
deep Which multimodal tasks admit verifiable rewards, and what are the hacking modes?▶
Verifiable: OCR (exact-match vs ground-truth text), derendering (re-render & compare / table exact-match), grounding (IoU / pointing), chart & doc QA (run the extracted code, numeric exact-match), counting. Hacking modes: satisfying exact-match via formatting tricks, "renders similar but structurally wrong," gaming an IoU threshold, or exploiting a lenient parser. Mitigate with structural checks (not appearance), multiple metrics, and held-out human spot-checks. The verifiability advantage is real but the checker is still attackable — route every capability through its most verifiable formulation and harvest that signal for RLVR + self-improvement.

§8 — Vertical integration & agentic capabilities

Résumé: "Oversee/advise MM side of vertically-integrated agentic capabilities: UI control, health, geo, MM document, Waymo foundational model, XR."

Mock — what "I" did I advised the multimodal side of agentic products — making sure the perception capabilities (read the screen, the document, the map, the medical image) are good enough to act on, and that product needs flow back into the pretraining data/capability roadmap. The through-line is that agents that act in the visual world are perception-bound: a UI agent fails if it can't read tiny text; a maps agent fails if it can't ground places.
implementation What does a screen/UI-control agent need from the perception stack?▶
High-resolution, native-aspect OCR (UI text is tiny — drives the encoder-heavy/resolution argument, Day 4 §1.5/§1.8), accurate 2D grounding (where is the button? → pointing/box tokens, §5), an action vocabulary (click/type/scroll grounded to coordinates), and robustness to dynamic layouts. It's a VLM driving a screen (Day 4 §3.6) — so it also needs agent reliability (error recovery, prompt-injection defense for untrusted page content) and is trained increasingly with RL on verifiable task success. The perception bar is the binding constraint: most failures are "couldn't read/locate it," not "couldn't plan."
problems & future What's the risk profile of medical / autonomous-driving multimodal agents?▶
High-stakes, low-tolerance domains: calibration and abstention matter more than peak accuracy (better to say "I can't tell" than hallucinate a diagnosis or a road agent's intent), distribution shift is safety-critical (rare events dominate risk), and you need verifiable/grounded outputs and human-in-the-loop for irreversible actions. For driving foundation models specifically, perception feeds prediction feeds planning, and errors compound — so eval must be long-tail and safety-weighted, not average-case (ties to my Uber ATG work, §9). Future: uncertainty-aware multimodal models and verifiable safety guarantees.
nuggets & taste Why does "vertical integration" change the research priorities?▶
Because real products surface the actual capability gaps — and they're almost always perception, not language. Owning the loop from product back to data means the roadmap is driven by "what eval does this unlock" rather than benchmark-chasing. The taste point: I let downstream agentic needs define the perception capabilities (UI text → resolution; maps → geo-grounding; docs → derendering) and feed those as targeted data/eval investments — which is more efficient than scaling blindly and hoping the capability emerges.

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep GUI agents on high-res screens — the token-cost problem and fixes.▶
A full-resolution screenshot at native res is thousands of visual tokens (the dynamic-resolution cost), and an agent loop re-encodes the screen every step → cost compounds over a long trajectory and the context overflows. Fixes: spatiotemporal token pruning (drop unchanged regions between steps), tiling with attention focused on the active region, caching the static UI, and set-of-marks / accessibility-tree shortcuts so you don't rely purely on pixels. The binding constraint is reading tiny UI text — which is exactly why high-resolution / encoder-heavy perception (§4) matters for agents.
deep Safety for medical / autonomous multimodal — what's different from chat?▶
Worst-case, not average-case, requirements: calibration + abstention ("I can't read this / I'm not sure" beats a confident wrong diagnosis or intent prediction), long-tail / distribution-shift eval (rare events dominate risk — the ATG lesson, §9), human-in-the-loop for irreversible actions, and worst-case / adversarial robustness (the UTRC lesson, §10 — jailbreaks/prompt-injection are the modern adversarial attack). Certified guarantees (randomized smoothing) and uncertainty quantification matter more than peak benchmark accuracy. The taste: in high-stakes domains, average metrics don't bound the failures that actually hurt.

§9 — Uber ATG: onboard perception & prediction

Résumé: "ML for autonomous driving — perception & prediction; pre-transformer CNN & GNN; safety-critical algorithms with custom CUDA kernels."

Mock — what "I" did I built onboard perception and prediction models for self-driving: detecting and tracking agents (vehicles/pedestrians/cyclists) from LiDAR + camera, and predicting their future trajectories — with CNNs for the perception backbone and GNNs for multi-agent interaction modeling, all under hard real-time latency budgets, which is why I wrote custom CUDA kernels for the hot paths.
implementation Why GNNs for prediction, and how do you model multi-agent interaction?▶
Trajectory prediction is multi-agent and interactive — what a car does depends on its neighbors. Model the scene as a graph: nodes = agents (and map elements/lanes as nodes too), edges = interactions; message passing aggregates neighbor states so each agent's prediction conditions on the others (VectorNet/LaneGCN lineage). Output is usually multi-modal (several plausible futures with probabilities — a car might turn or go straight), trained with a winner-take-all / mixture loss to avoid mode-averaging. Pre-transformer, GNNs were the natural relational inductive bias; today attention over agents/lanes does the same job (the same "tokens attend to tokens" idea).
implementation Why custom CUDA kernels, and where?▶
Onboard inference has a hard real-time latency budget (the planner needs predictions every cycle) on embedded automotive hardware. Generic framework ops were too slow/memory-heavy for the hot paths, so you write custom kernels for: voxelization / point-cloud scatter-gather (irregular memory), BEV (bird's-eye-view) feature pooling, sparse convolutions, and the GNN message-passing aggregation. The wins come from fusing ops (avoid HBM round-trips — the same IO lesson as FlashAttention, Day 5 §3.3), exploiting sparsity, and tuning for the target GPU. Safety-critical means deterministic latency, not just average throughput.
problems & future What's the hard part of safety-critical prediction, and how does it connect to your Gemini work?▶
The long tail: rare events (jaywalkers, occluded agents, unusual maneuvers) dominate risk but are sparse in data, so average-case metrics lie — you need safety-weighted, scenario-based eval and calibrated uncertainty (predict distributions, know when you don't know). It connects directly to my multimodal physical-intelligence and AV-foundation-model work (§5, §8): the field is moving from hand-engineered perception→prediction pipelines to large multimodal foundation models that do perception, prediction, and planning more end-to-end — but the safety, long-tail, and calibration lessons from ATG are exactly what those foundation models still need.
nuggets & taste What did AV teach you that transfers to frontier LLM/MM work?▶
Three things: (1) the long tail is the whole game — average metrics hide the failures that matter (same as eval design, Day 3/5); (2) calibration/abstention beats overconfident accuracy in high-stakes settings (same as hallucination in VLMs); (3) systems and ML are inseparable — the IO/latency discipline of CUDA kernels is the same lesson as FlashAttention and KV-cache economics. The relational inductive bias of GNNs → attention is also a clean example of the field generalizing a specific structure into a universal one.

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep Rasterized-BEV CNN vs VectorNet/LaneGCN — the real tradeoff, with numbers.▶
Rasterize the scene (agents + HD map) into a top-down BEV image and run a CNN — simple, conv-hardware-friendly, but lossy (rasterization discretizes geometry) and wastes compute on empty space. Vectorized: represent lanes/agent-histories as polylines → graph. VectorNet uses a hierarchical local graph over vector sets (~28% error reduction at the 3-s horizon by adding map polylines). LaneGCN builds an explicit lane graph (ActorNet → MapNet → FusionNet → prediction header) and cut 6-s error 1.90 → 1.35 m on Argoverse. Vectorized preserves geometry and is efficient but more complex. Today, attention over agent/lane tokens (the transformer) subsumes both — the same "tokens attend to tokens" relational bias, now learned.
deep Multimodal trajectory prediction — the mixture / winner-take-all loss and its failure.▶
A car might turn or go straight, so predict $K$ modes with probabilities. A naive average-over-modes loss → mode averaging (a blurred path straight through the curve). Fix: winner-take-all / "min-of-K" — backprop the regression loss only through the mode closest to ground truth (multiple-choice learning), so modes specialize, plus a classification loss on mode probability. Failures: mode collapse (all $K$ converge) and miscalibrated probabilities; mitigations include diversity regularization and anchor/target trajectories (MultiPath / TNT). Report minADE / minFDE + miss-rate, not average error — average hides whether the right mode was covered.
deep Which CUDA kernels did you actually write, and what did you fuse?▶
The hot, irregular ops generic frameworks handle poorly: voxelization / point-cloud scatter (irregular gather/scatter onto a sparse grid), sparse / submanifold convolution (compute only at occupied voxels — dense conv wastes 90%+ on empty space), BEV pooling, and GNN message-passing aggregation (gather neighbor features). The wins come from fusing ops to avoid HBM round-trips (the same IO lesson as FlashAttention, Day 5) and exploiting sparsity. Safety-critical means optimizing deterministic worst-case latency, not just average throughput — the planner needs a prediction every cycle, every cycle.

§10 — UTRC: generative signal models, PGMs, adversarial robustness

Résumé: "PI on DoD/DOE programs — LSTM-based SignalGAN; indoor-occupancy probabilistic graphical models; adversarial robustness of DNNs."

Mock — what "I" did As PI on applied-research programs I built: a SignalGAN (LSTM-based GAN) to model/generate time-series sensor signals (data augmentation / anomaly detection); probabilistic graphical models for indoor occupancy estimation (inferring how many people are where from sparse sensors); and studied adversarial robustness of deep nets (how small perturbations fool them, and defenses).
implementation Why an LSTM-based GAN for signals, and what's tricky about it?▶
Sensor signals are sequential, so generator and discriminator are recurrent (LSTM) to capture temporal structure (cf. C-RNN-GAN / TimeGAN). Uses: data augmentation for scarce labeled regimes, anomaly detection (low discriminator score = anomaly), and simulation. Tricky parts: GAN training instability (mode collapse, non-convergence — needs tricks like feature matching, gradient penalty/WGAN, careful LR balancing), evaluating generated signals (no FID equivalent — use downstream-task or distributional metrics), and long-sequence credit assignment through the recurrence. The modern lens: diffusion/transformer sequence models have largely superseded GANs for generation, but the adversarial idea persists (e.g., discriminators as learned rewards).
implementation What's the PGM formulation for indoor occupancy?▶
Model occupancy as latent variables (number/location of people per zone) generating noisy sensor observations (CO₂, motion, power, Wi-Fi), with a graphical model encoding spatial/temporal structure — e.g. an HMM/dynamic Bayesian network over time, or an MRF over rooms. Inference (belief propagation / variational / particle filtering) gives a posterior over occupancy with calibrated uncertainty — the advantage of PGMs over a black-box regressor when data is scarce and you need interpretable, uncertainty-aware estimates with physical priors baked in (sensor models, conservation constraints).
problems & future What did adversarial robustness teach that's still relevant?▶
That deep nets are brittle to worst-case inputs (tiny $\ell_p$ perturbations flip predictions — FGSM/PGD attacks), defended imperfectly by adversarial training (train on attacks; expensive, trades clean accuracy) and certified methods. Still relevant: it's the security-mindset ancestor of jailbreaks and prompt injection in LLMs/agents (Day 4 §3.5) — worst-case, adversarial robustness rather than average-case accuracy. The taste lesson: average-case metrics don't bound worst-case behavior, which matters for safety-critical and agentic systems.
nuggets & taste How do you connect this early work to today's frontier?▶
A coherent arc: generative modeling (GANs → diffusion/AR), probabilistic/uncertainty reasoning (PGMs → calibration & Bayesian deep learning), and adversarial robustness (→ jailbreak/injection security) are all alive in frontier multimodal systems — just scaled up and unified into one model. The throughline in my work is uncertainty-aware, robust, generative modeling of structured signals, which is exactly what grounded multimodal understanding and physical intelligence require. I'd frame the early work not as "old" but as the foundations whose lessons (long tail, calibration, worst-case, generation-as-understanding) I now apply at frontier scale.

Deeper — only-the-doers-know

deep Why are GANs unstable on sequences, and what replaced them?▶
GAN training is a non-stationary minimax game — no single loss monotonically descends — so you get mode collapse, vanishing discriminator gradients, and oscillation; recurrence (LSTM) adds long-horizon credit assignment on top. Stabilizers: feature matching, WGAN-GP (Wasserstein distance + gradient penalty), and TimeGAN (combine the adversarial loss with a supervised stepwise prediction loss in a learned embedding space to capture temporal dynamics). The modern replacement: diffusion and transformer sequence models give higher fidelity and far more stable training — but the adversarial idea persists as discriminators-as-learned-rewards and in the score/energy-model connection.
deep Empirical vs certified adversarial robustness — the tradeoff.▶
Attack: PGD = iterated FGSM projected back into an ℓp ball — the standard white-box benchmark. Empirical defense: PGD adversarial training (Madry — train on worst-case perturbations; strong but expensive and trades clean accuracy) and TRADES (a KL term explicitly trading natural vs robust error). Certified defense: randomized smoothing (classify under Gaussian noise by majority vote → a provable ℓ₂ radius; Cohen et al. 2019 — the only certified method shown to scale to full-resolution ImageNet) and IBP / convex relaxations for ReLU nets. Tradeoff: empirical is strong but unverified (adaptive attacks can break it); certified gives guarantees but smaller radii and an accuracy cost. The enduring lesson: average accuracy ≠ worst-case safety — the direct ancestor of LLM jailbreak / prompt-injection robustness (Day 4 §3.5).
deep PGM occupancy — which inference method, and why?▶
Model occupancy as latent states emitting noisy sensor readings (CO₂ / motion / power) with temporal structure (HMM / dynamic Bayes net) or spatial structure (MRF over rooms). Inference choice by graph: exact belief propagation if tree-like / low-treewidth; loopy BP or variational inference for loopy graphs (fast, biased); particle filtering for nonlinear / non-Gaussian dynamics (handles multimodal posteriors, costlier). You pick by structure and the need for calibrated uncertainty — the PGM's advantage over a black-box regressor is an interpretable posterior with physical priors (sensor models, conservation) baked in, which matters when data is scarce and decisions are safety-relevant.
