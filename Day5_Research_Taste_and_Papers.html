The research round rewards structured judgment under uncertainty. Everything here is about producing that on demand.

§1 — What "research taste" actually is

Research taste is the set of judgments you make under uncertainty: what problem is worth attacking, which hypothesis is most likely true, what is the cheapest experiment that would change your mind, what evidence you'd accept, and when to stop, scale, or pivot. Interviewers can't test taste by asking facts — they test it by asking open questions and listening to how you reason.

It decomposes into five habits, and every drill in this doc trains one of them:

HabitThe question it answersSignal of good taste
Problem selectionIs this worth working on now?High impact × tractable × differentiated; aware of what scaling will solve for free.
Hypothesis qualityWhat do I actually believe, and why?A falsifiable claim with a mechanism, not a vibe.
Experimental designWhat's the cheapest test that moves my belief most?Isolates one variable; compute-matched; has a clear decision rule.
Evidence standardsDo I believe this number?Asks about baselines, seeds/variance, contamination, confounds before celebrating.
Knowing the next moveScale, ablate, pivot, or ship?Reads the result and chooses the action with the best information-per-compute.

1.1 The weak → strong answer pattern (internalize this)

For any question, answers live on a ladder. Weak answers recite; strong answers reason about tradeoffs, propose a test, and tie back to compute/data economics. Climb the ladder out loud:

Most candidates stop at L1–L2. Taste is visible at L3–L5: tradeoffs, a concrete experiment, and a falsification condition. You don't need to be right — you need to reason like someone who finds out.

⚙ The reusable closing move End open answers with one sentence that names the experiment and the decision rule: "The cheapest way to find out is [experiment], and I'd believe [hypothesis] if [measurable outcome] — otherwise I'd conclude [alternative]." This single habit lifts almost any answer two rungs.

1.2 The taste rubric — what the interviewer is scoring

  • Compute-awareness: do you reason about FLOPs, data, and inference cost, or only accuracy? (Days 2–3.)
  • Falsifiability: can you state what would change your mind?
  • Skepticism without cynicism: you probe a claim's evidence but still know how to make progress.
  • Connection across areas: "this is the same Goodhart problem as evals," "KV-cache cost is why GQA exists" — you see the field as one graph.
  • Calibration: you say how confident you are and why; you distinguish "I know" from "I'd bet" from "I'd test."

§2 — The 5-point paper deep-dive frame

You'll be asked "tell me about a paper you like." A strong answer is not a summary — it's a structured judgment. Use the same five points for every paper, and you'll never freeze:

  1. Problem. What concrete thing was broken/expensive/impossible before? (One sentence.)
  2. Key idea. The single insight, stated so a smart non-expert gets it.
  3. Method. The mechanism, precisely — the equation or the algorithm, with notation defined.
  4. Evidence. What experiment makes the claim credible? Is the baseline fair, compute-matched, ablated? What's the strongest result and the most suspicious one?
  5. Limitations & what's next. Where it breaks, what it assumes, and the experiment you'd run next. This is the taste payload.
◆ How to critique (the believe-it checklist, short form) Is the baseline strong and compute-matched? Are gains within noise (seeds/variance)? Could contamination explain it (Day 3)? Is the improvement a confound (more data/params/tuning) rather than the named cause? Did they ablate the one component they credit? Would it survive at scale? (Full framework in §4.)

§3 — Anchor papers (the canon you must be able to deep-dive)

Six papers cover most of what a frontier interviewer will probe. For each: problem → key idea → method (with math) → evidence → limitations → "what to say." Master these and you can frame almost anything by analogy.

3.1 Transformer — Attention Is All You Need (2017)

Problem. RNNs process sequences sequentially — no parallelism over time, and long-range dependencies pass through many steps (vanishing signal). Key idea. Replace recurrence with self-attention: every token directly attends to every other in one parallel operation, so path length between any two tokens is $O(1)$. Method:

$$\text{Attention}(Q,K,V)=\operatorname{softmax}\!\Big(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\Big)V$$

Notation $Q=XW_Q,\,K=XW_K,\,V=XW_V$ are the queries / keys / values ($X\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$ the token embeddings, $n$ tokens, $d$ model width). $QK^\top\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ scores every token pair; $\sqrt{d_k}$ rescales so the dot product has unit variance (stops softmax saturation, Day 2); softmax makes each row a distribution; multiplying by $V$ returns a weighted average of values — a content-based lookup. Multi-head runs $h$ of these in parallel subspaces.

Evidence. SOTA translation at a fraction of the training cost, and — the real payload — it scales: the architecture's parallelism made today's LLMs trainable. Limitations. $O(n^2)$ attention compute/memory (the long-context wall, Day 2 § long-context → FlashAttention §3.3); no built-in recurrence/state. What to say: "The lasting contribution isn't translation quality — it's an architecture whose compute is dense, parallel, and hardware-friendly, so it kept paying off as we scaled $N$ and $D$. That's why 'attention is all you need' was an understatement."

3.2 Chinchilla — compute-optimal scaling (2022)

Problem. Given a fixed compute budget $C$, how should you split it between model size $N$ (parameters) and data $D$ (tokens)? Pre-Chinchilla models (GPT-3, Gopher) were too big and under-trained. Key idea. There's a compute-optimal frontier: for each $C$, scale $N$ and $D$ together — roughly 20 tokens per parameter. Method. Fit a parametric loss surface and minimize it under the compute constraint $C\approx 6ND$:

$$L(N,D)=\underbrace{E}_{\text{irreducible}}+\underbrace{\frac{A}{N^{\alpha}}}_{\text{finite model}}+\underbrace{\frac{B}{D^{\beta}}}_{\text{finite data}},\qquad \min_{N,D} L \ \text{ s.t. }\ C=6ND$$

Notation $L$ = test cross-entropy (nats/token); $E$ = irreducible entropy of language; $A,B,\alpha,\beta$ = fitted constants ($\alpha\approx\beta\approx0.34$). The two power-law terms are the cost of a finite model and a finite dataset. $C=6ND$ is the training-FLOP rule (6 FLOPs per parameter per token). Solving the constrained minimization gives $N_\text{opt}\propto C^{0.5}$ and $D_\text{opt}\propto C^{0.5}$ — both grow as $\sqrt{C}$, i.e. equally.

IsoFLOP view (the method that found the law): at each compute budget, sweep model size; loss is U-shaped (too small underfits; too big is under-trained for that budget). The minimum is compute-optimal; the locus of minima is the frontier $N_\text{opt}(C)$. Chinchilla (70B/1.4T) beat Gopher (280B/300B tokens) at equal compute.

Evidence. Three independent estimation methods agreed; the 70B Chinchilla beat the 280B Gopher at equal compute. Limitations / nuance. The law is for training loss-optimal allocation; if you'll serve the model a lot, over-train a smaller model past compute-optimal to cut inference cost (the Llama/inference-aware regime). The constants are dataset- and tokenizer-dependent. What to say: "Chinchilla reframed scaling as an allocation problem and showed everyone was under-training. The modern caveat is inference-awareness: compute-optimal for training ≠ optimal for total cost of ownership."

3.3 FlashAttention — IO-aware exact attention (2022)

Problem (told properly). A GPU has two kinds of memory: a huge but slow main memory (HBM, ~40–80 GB, ~2–3 TB/s) and a tiny but fast on-chip scratchpad (SRAM, ~20 MB, ~19 TB/s — roughly 10× faster). Standard attention computes the full $n\times n$ score matrix $S=QK^\top$, writes all of it to HBM, reads it back to do softmax, writes the softmax matrix back, reads it again to multiply by $V$. For a 8k-token sequence that matrix is 64M numbers — and you move it across the slow HBM bus several times. The GPU's math units sit idle waiting for data: attention is memory-bandwidth-bound, not compute-bound. The wasted resource is data movement, not FLOPs.

Key idea. Never write the $n\times n$ matrix to HBM at all. Cut $Q,K,V$ into blocks small enough to fit in SRAM; for each block of queries, stream the key/value blocks through SRAM, accumulating the output on the fly. The only thing written back to HBM is the final output $O$ (size $n\times d$, not $n\times n$). HBM traffic drops from $O(n^2)$ to $O(n)$.

The one obstacle, and the fix. Softmax needs the whole row to normalize ($\frac{e^{s_j}}{\sum_k e^{s_k}}$ needs the sum over all keys), but we only have one key-block in SRAM at a time. The fix is online (streaming) softmax: keep a running maximum $m$ and a running denominator $\ell$, and retro-actively rescale what you've accumulated whenever a new block reveals a larger max.

Worked micro-example — online softmax of [1, 3] in two chunks Goal: softmax-weight values $v_a$ (logit 1) and $v_b$ (logit 3), but see them one at a time.
Chunk 1 (logit 1): running max $m=1$, denom $\ell=e^{1-1}=1$, output $O=\tfrac{e^{0}}{1}v_a=v_a$.
Chunk 2 (logit 3): new max $m'=3$. Rescale the old state by $e^{m-m'}=e^{1-3}=e^{-2}$: $\ell' = e^{-2}\cdot 1 + e^{3-3}=e^{-2}+1$, and $O' = \tfrac{e^{-2}\cdot \ell_{\text{old}}}{\ell'}O + \tfrac{e^{0}}{\ell'}v_b$.
Final weights: $v_a$ gets $\tfrac{e^{-2}}{e^{-2}+1}\approx0.12$, $v_b$ gets $\tfrac{1}{e^{-2}+1}\approx0.88$ — exactly $\text{softmax}([1,3])$. The $e^{m-m'}$ factor is the whole trick: it corrects earlier partial sums the instant a bigger logit appears, so streaming gives the same answer as seeing the row all at once.

Generalized to blocks (block $i$ over streamed key blocks): running max $m_i=\max(m_{i-1},\tilde m_i)$; running denom $\ell_i=e^{m_{i-1}-m_i}\ell_{i-1}+e^{\tilde m_i-m_i}\tilde\ell_i$; running output $O_i=\tfrac{e^{m_{i-1}-m_i}\ell_{i-1}}{\ell_i}O_{i-1}+(\text{contribution of block }i)$, where $\tilde m_i,\tilde\ell_i$ are the current block's local max and exp-sum. Because every step is an exact algebraic rescale, the result is bit-for-bit the true softmax — it's not an approximation.

Evidence. 2–4× wall-clock speedup, exact (not an approximation), and it made long context practical; now the default kernel everywhere. FlashAttention-2/3 further improved work-partitioning and used newer GPU features. Limitations. Compute is still $O(n^2 d)$ FLOPs (long context is fundamentally expensive — this fixes memory, not the quadratic compute), and the kernels are hardware-specific. What to say: "The lesson is that the bottleneck was IO, not FLOPs — attention was bandwidth-bound, so the win came from restructuring the computation to respect the memory hierarchy (keep things in SRAM, never spill the $n^2$ matrix to HBM), made possible by online softmax. And crucially it's exact, which is why it beat the dozens of approximate-attention methods that traded accuracy for speed."

3.4 RLHF → DPO — aligning to preferences (2022–2023)

Problem. Many goals (helpfulness, harmlessness) are easier to compare than to demonstrate — and easier to compare than to write a reward for. Key idea (RLHF). Learn a reward model $r_\phi$ from human preference pairs, then RL-optimize the policy against it with a KL leash to stay near the SFT model. Key idea (DPO). You don't need the RL loop or a separate reward model at all — the KL-regularized objective has a closed-form optimum, which turns preference learning into a simple classification loss on the policy itself.

RLHF objective and the Bradley–Terry reward model:

$$\max_{\pi_\theta}\ \mathbb{E}_{x,\,y\sim\pi_\theta}\big[r_\phi(x,y)\big]-\beta\,\mathrm{KL}\!\big(\pi_\theta(\cdot|x)\,\|\,\pi_\text{ref}(\cdot|x)\big),\qquad P(y_w\!\succ\!y_l)=\sigma\big(r_\phi(x,y_w)-r_\phi(x,y_l)\big)$$

DPO substitutes the closed-form optimal policy back in, eliminating $r_\phi$:

$$\mathcal{L}_\text{DPO}=-\,\mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)}\Big[\log\sigma\Big(\beta\log\tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_w|x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y_w|x)}-\beta\log\tfrac{\pi_\theta(y_l|x)}{\pi_\text{ref}(y_l|x)}\Big)\Big]$$

Notation $\pi_\theta$ = the policy (model) being trained; $\pi_\text{ref}$ = frozen SFT reference; $x$ = prompt; $y_w,y_l$ = preferred ("win") and dispreferred ("lose") responses; $r_\phi$ = reward model; $\sigma$ = logistic sigmoid; $\beta$ = KL strength (how far the policy may drift). The DPO term inside $\sigma$ is the implicit reward $\hat r=\beta\log(\pi_\theta/\pi_\text{ref})$ — DPO raises the implicit reward of $y_w$ over $y_l$, which is exactly the Bradley–Terry objective without an explicit $r_\phi$ or sampling.

Evidence. RLHF made GPT-style assistants usable; DPO matches or beats PPO-RLHF on many preference benchmarks while being far simpler/cheaper (no reward model, no online sampling). Limitations. DPO is offline (fixed preference data) → can't explore; can over-fit / drift on out-of-distribution prompts; the KL leash and reward hacking still bite RLHF. What to say: "DPO's elegance is recognizing that the RL objective has a closed-form solution, so you can skip the reward model and the unstable online loop. The cost is losing exploration — which is exactly why online/iterative DPO and back-to-PPO show up when you need the policy to discover behaviors, not just rank fixed ones." (Full mechanics: Day 6.)

3.5 GRPO / RLVR — RL for reasoning (2024–2025)

Problem. Preference RL optimizes style; it doesn't directly teach correctness on tasks with checkable answers (math, code). Key idea. Use verifiable rewards (RLVR) — the reward is 1 if the answer passes a checker (unit tests, exact match) — and a critic-free policy-gradient method, GRPO, that estimates advantage by normalizing rewards within a group of sampled answers to the same prompt. Method:

$$\hat A_i=\frac{r_i-\operatorname{mean}(r_1,\dots,r_G)}{\operatorname{std}(r_1,\dots,r_G)},\qquad \mathcal{J}_\text{GRPO}=\mathbb{E}\Big[\tfrac1G\textstyle\sum_i \min\big(\rho_i\hat A_i,\ \operatorname{clip}(\rho_i,1\!-\!\epsilon,1\!+\!\epsilon)\hat A_i\big)\Big]-\beta\,\mathrm{KL}$$

Notation For one prompt, sample a group of $G$ completions; $r_i$ = (verifiable) reward of completion $i$; $\hat A_i$ = its group-normalized advantage (z-score within the group — no learned value/critic network needed, which is GRPO's whole simplification vs PPO); $\rho_i=\pi_\theta/\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}$ = importance ratio; $\operatorname{clip}$ + $\epsilon$ = PPO-style trust region; $\beta\,\mathrm{KL}$ = leash to the reference. Intuition: "make the above-average answers in this group more likely, the below-average ones less."

Evidence. Drove the 2025 reasoning wave (DeepSeek-R1-style): large gains on math/code with pure RL from verifiable rewards, and emergent long chain-of-thought. Limitations. Needs a verifier (limits domains); reward hacking the checker; can collapse diversity; group sampling is compute-heavy. What to say: "RLVR works because the reward is unhackable in principle — it's ground truth — and GRPO drops the critic by using the group as its own baseline. The frontier question is extending verifiable reward beyond math/code to fuzzy domains." (Full derivation chain: Day 6.)

3.6 CLIP — contrastive vision-language pretraining (2021)

Problem. Vision models needed labeled datasets per task; no open-vocabulary, no zero-shot. Key idea. Learn a shared image–text embedding space from cheap web (image, caption) pairs with a contrastive loss, enabling zero-shot classification by comparing an image to text prompts. Method. Symmetric InfoNCE over the $N\times N$ cosine-similarity matrix of a batch (full treatment in Day 4 §1.1):

$$\mathcal{L}=\tfrac12\Big[-\tfrac1N\textstyle\sum_i\log\frac{e^{s_{ii}/\tau}}{\sum_j e^{s_{ij}/\tau}}-\tfrac1N\sum_j\log\frac{e^{s_{jj}/\tau}}{\sum_i e^{s_{ij}/\tau}}\Big],\quad s_{ij}=\frac{I_i\cdot T_j}{\lVert I_i\rVert\lVert T_j\rVert}$$

Notation$N$ = batch size; $I_i,T_j$ = L2-normalized image/text embeddings; $s_{ij}$ = cosine similarity; $\tau$ = learned temperature; diagonal = true pairs, off-diagonal = in-batch negatives. More negatives (bigger batch) = a harder, more informative task.

Evidence. Strong zero-shot transfer across dozens of datasets from web supervision; became the default vision backbone for VLMs. Limitations. Weak at counting/spatial/fine detail; data-distribution-dependent; the embedding is global (poor dense features → pair with DINOv2, Day 4 §1.10). What to say: "CLIP's lesson is that natural-language supervision scales: captions are a cheap, open-vocabulary label source, and the contrastive objective turns them into a transferable representation. Its limits (no generation, weak dense features) are exactly what CoCa, SigLIP, and DINO addressed next."

3.7 Recent papers — "good-taste" deep-dives (2024–2026)

When they ask "a paper you like," a recent pick signals you're reading the current literature. Here are four, each in the 5-point frame with the taste angle — the non-obvious "why it matters" that separates a summary from a judgment.

◆ DeepSeek-R1 / R1-Zero — RL incentivizes reasoning (2025) Problem: reasoning was taught mostly by SFT on human/curated chains-of-thought — expensive and capped by the demonstrations. Key idea: you can elicit reasoning with pure RL from verifiable rewards, no SFT at all. Method: R1-Zero starts from the base model and runs GRPO with a rule-based reward = accuracy (is the final answer right, checked by a math verifier or code tests) + a format reward (put thinking in <think> tags). No reward model, no human labels. Evidence: long chains-of-thought, self-reflection, and "aha moments" (the model spontaneously re-checks its work) emerge; large math/code gains. R1 then adds a little SFT cold-start for readability. Limitations: needs verifiable domains; R1-Zero's raw output is unreadable/mixed-language; diversity can collapse. Taste angle: "the striking claim is that the capability was latent in the base model and RL just surfaced it — pretraining built the reasoning, RL learned to use it. And it works because the reward is ground truth, not a learned proxy, so there's nothing to hack. The open question is extending verifiable reward beyond math/code."
◆ DeepSeek-V3 — frontier quality via efficiency co-design (2024) Problem: frontier models are ruinously expensive to train and serve; the KV cache and dense FFNs dominate cost. Key idea: get frontier quality by co-designing the architecture for efficiency, not by brute scale. Method (the four moves to name): (1) MLA (Multi-head Latent Attention) — compress the keys/values into a small latent vector, so the KV cache shrinks dramatically (attacks the Day-2 KV-cache wall); (2) DeepSeekMoE — many fine-grained experts + a few always-on shared experts (671B params, only ~37B active per token); (3) auxiliary-loss-free load balancing — balance expert usage with a learned per-expert bias instead of a balancing loss that hurts quality; (4) multi-token prediction (predict several next tokens → denser signal + speculative decoding) and FP8 training. Evidence: GPT-4-class quality at a fraction of the training cost; strong open weights. Limitations: MoE serving/memory complexity; FP8 stability engineering. Taste angle: "the lesson is that systems and modeling are inseparable at the frontier — the wins are architectural efficiency (MLA on the KV cache, fine-grained MoE on FLOPs), not raw scale. It's the most concrete recent example of Chinchilla's successor question: optimize total cost of ownership, not just training loss."
◆ Mamba — selective state-space models (2023–24) Problem: attention is $O(n^2)$ and its KV cache grows with context; can we model sequences in linear time with constant state? Key idea: a selective state-space model — a learned recurrence whose transition parameters are input-dependent (the "selection" mechanism), so it can choose what to remember/forget, recovering much of what made attention powerful. Method: an SSM $h_t=\bar A h_{t-1}+\bar B x_t,\ y_t=Ch_t$ with $\bar A,\bar B$ functions of the input; a hardware-aware parallel scan makes it fast on GPUs. Linear time, $O(1)$ state at inference (no growing KV cache). Evidence: matches/beats Transformers at similar scale on language and excels on very long sequences (audio, genomics). Limitations: weaker at exact in-context recall/copying (attention's random-access is genuinely useful), so the field moved to hybrids (mostly-Mamba + a few attention layers). Taste angle: "the key insight is selectivity — earlier linear-attention/SSM models failed because they were input-independent; making the recurrence content-aware closed most of the gap. But the honest read is that attention's exact recall is hard to give up, which is why the winning recipe is a hybrid, not a replacement."
◆ Test-time compute scaling — the new axis (o1 / R1 era, 2024–25) Problem: the train-time scaling laws (Chinchilla) are hitting data/compute limits; is there another axis? Key idea: spend more compute at inference — let the model "think" longer (long CoT, sampling + verification, search) — and accuracy keeps rising, an inference-time scaling law. Method: RL-train models to produce long reasoning traces (R1/o1), then at test time scale via longer chains, best-of-n with a verifier, or self-consistency; on some tasks a small model + lots of test-time compute beats a big model decoded once. Evidence: steep accuracy-vs-inference-compute curves on math/code/reasoning. Limitations: only helps where extra thinking pays (verifiable/reasoning tasks), and inference cost balloons. Taste angle: "this reframes 'scaling' — there are now two knobs, train-time and test-time, and the compute-optimal frontier is a tradeoff between them. It shifts cost from training to serving, which changes the economics (you pay per query), and it's why 'reasoning models' are a distinct product tier."
⚙ How to open the answer Pick one and lead with the taste angle, not the abstract: "I like DeepSeek-R1 because it shows the reasoning capability was latent in the base model and pure RL with verifiable rewards surfaced it — which reframes post-training as eliciting rather than teaching." Then offer to go deeper on method/evidence. For multimodal picks, use SigLIP 2, Qwen2-VL (M-RoPE), Apple's native-multimodal scaling, or Janus/Transfusion (Day 4 / resume-prep doc).

Self-test §3

State Chinchilla's result and its modern caveat.▶
At fixed compute, scale $N$ and $D$ together (~20 tokens/param); prior models were under-trained. Caveat: that's training-optimal — if you serve heavily, over-train a smaller model past compute-optimal to cut inference cost.
Why does DPO avoid a reward model?▶
The KL-regularized RLHF objective has a closed-form optimal policy; substituting it turns preference learning into a logistic loss on $\beta\log(\pi_\theta/\pi_\text{ref})$ — the implicit reward. No $r_\phi$, no online RL. Cost: it's offline (no exploration).
What does FlashAttention actually optimize, and is it exact?▶
HBM memory traffic ($O(n^2)\to O(n)$) via tiling + online softmax — attention was bandwidth-bound, not FLOP-bound. It's exact, not an approximation; compute stays $O(n^2 d)$.
Why is GRPO "critic-free," and why RLVR?▶
GRPO replaces a learned value function with the group mean as the baseline (z-score advantage within $G$ samples). RLVR uses verifiable rewards (checker = 1/0) so the reward is ground-truth and hard to hack — ideal for math/code.

§4 — "Do you believe this result?" — a framework

The single most common research-round move: present a result, watch you evaluate it. Run this checklist out loud — it's the operationalization of evidence standards.

  1. Strong, compute-matched baseline? The #1 way papers mislead — a weak/under-tuned baseline. Gains vs a strong baseline at equal compute are the only ones that count.
  2. Within noise? 1 seed and +0.3% is nothing. Ask for variance / multiple seeds / confidence intervals.
  3. Contamination? Could the test set (or near-duplicates) be in training? Especially for public benchmarks (Day 3 §2).
  4. Confound vs cause? Did the method also bring more data, parameters, or tuning? Then the named mechanism may not be why.
  5. Ablation? Did they remove just the credited component and show the gain disappears? No ablation → no causal claim.
  6. Scale? Many effects vanish (or invert) at scale. Would you bet it survives 10×? (Some things scaling fixes for free — don't work on those.)
⚙ The one-liner to deploy "Before I believe +2%, I'd want a compute-matched strong baseline, variance across seeds, a contamination check, and an ablation that removes the one component they credit — and I'd ask whether it survives at scale."

§5 — Proposing research (do this out loud)

"How would you improve X?" wants a structured proposal, not a brainstorm. Use this skeleton; it mirrors how you'd actually scope a project:

  1. Motivation: what's broken and why it matters (impact).
  2. Hypothesis: a falsifiable claim with a mechanism ("I believe Z because…").
  3. Minimal experiment: the cheapest test that would confirm/refute it — one variable, compute-matched, clear metric.
  4. Metric & decision rule: what you'd measure and the threshold to proceed.
  5. Risks & confounds: what could fool you; how you'd control it.
  6. Scaling plan: if the small test wins, how you'd ladder up (and what could break at scale).
★ Worked example — "improve long-context reasoning" Motivation: models degrade on mid-context info (lost-in-the-middle) and multi-hop reasoning over long inputs. Hypothesis: failures are retrieval-within-context failures, not reasoning failures — the model can reason if the relevant spans are surfaced. Minimal experiment: on a multi-hop long-context benchmark, compare (a) raw long context vs (b) same context with an in-context summary of relevant spans prepended — compute-matched, same model. Metric: accuracy on questions whose evidence sits mid-context; decision rule: proceed if (b) closes >50% of the gap to short-context. Risks: the summary leaks the answer (confound) → use an extraction step that can't see the question. Scaling: if it holds, train the behavior in (retrieval-augmented attention / learned span selection) and test at longer lengths. State which result would make you drop the idea.

14 research-taste drills

Answer each out loud, ~2–3 min, reaching L4–L5 (experiment + falsification). Then reveal a model answer.

Drill 1 — A new method beats the baseline by 2% on benchmark X. What do you check before believing it?▶
Run the believe-it checklist: (1) is the baseline strong and compute-matched (same params/data/tuning)? (2) is +2% within noise — how many seeds, what's the variance/CI? (3) contamination — could test data be in train, esp. a public benchmark? (4) confound — did the method also add data/params/tuning? (5) ablation — does the gain vanish when you remove only the credited component? (6) does it scale? Closing: "I'd believe it given a compute-matched baseline, seed variance, a contamination check, and an isolating ablation."
Drill 2 — Design an experiment to test whether data quality or quantity matters more.▶
Hold compute fixed and vary the axis: train compute-matched models on (a) the full noisy corpus of $D$ tokens vs (b) a quality-filtered subset of $D'\lt D$ tokens, optionally repeated to match tokens-seen. Also sweep a quality×quantity grid at one budget. Metric: held-out loss + downstream evals on a clean, contamination-checked set. Decision rule: quality wins if filtered-but-fewer beats full-but-noisy at equal compute (the FineWeb/DataComp finding). Confounds: filtering can change domain mix (control it); repetition has its own diminishing returns (cap epochs). Falsification: if (a)≈(b) across budgets, quantity dominates in this regime.
Drill 3 — Propose a direction to improve long-context reasoning.▶
Use the §5 skeleton. Hypothesis: long-context failures are in-context retrieval failures (lost-in-the-middle), not reasoning. Minimal test: compare raw long context vs context + surfaced relevant spans, compute-matched, measuring accuracy on mid-context-evidence questions; proceed if it closes >50% of the gap. Risk: the span-surfacer leaks the answer → make extraction blind to the question. Scale: if it holds, train learned span-selection / retrieval-augmented attention and test at longer lengths. Name the result that would kill the idea.
Drill 4 — A teammate wants to scale the model 10×. How do you decide if it's worth it?▶
Frame as information-per-compute. (1) Predict the gain with a scaling law fit at small scale (IsoFLOP / extrapolate $L(N,D)$) — does 10× move the metric you care about, or is it on the flat part? (2) Is the bottleneck capacity or data? If data-bound, 10× params won't help (Chinchilla) — get/clean data instead. (3) Inference cost: will you serve it? maybe over-train a smaller model instead. (4) Cheaper alternatives first: better data, post-training, architecture. Decision rule: scale only if the extrapolated gain on a real held-out metric beats the same compute spent on data/post-training.
Drill 5 — Your eval improved but users complain. What happened, what do you do?▶
Goodhart / metric–reality gap. Possible causes: the benchmark is contaminated or saturated; you optimized a proxy (e.g., perplexity, or an LLM-judge with length/sycophancy bias) that diverged from utility; distribution shift between eval and real prompts; reward hacking in post-training. Do: (1) collect real failing prompts, build a held-out, contamination-checked eval that mirrors usage; (2) check for proxy bias (length-controlled, human spot-checks); (3) A/B with humans. Lesson: "an eval is a proxy; when proxy and reality diverge, fix the proxy, don't trust the number."
Drill 6 — Is a capability "emergent," or an artifact of the metric?▶
Many "emergent jumps" are artifacts of discontinuous metrics (exact-match/accuracy) — switch to a smooth metric (per-token log-prob, partial credit, edit distance) and the jump often becomes a smooth curve (Schaeffer et al.). Test: plot the capability vs scale under both a hard and a soft metric; if the discontinuity disappears under the soft metric, it was measurement, not a phase change. Also check for contamination and prompt-format sensitivity. Taste: distinguish "the model genuinely couldn't, now can" from "our ruler couldn't see partial progress."
Drill 7 — You have 1000 H100s for a month to improve a 10B model. What do you do?▶
Spend the first slice on cheap diagnosis: is it data-, capacity-, or post-training-bound? Fit a small scaling law; inspect failures. Then allocate by expected info-per-compute, likely: (1) data — clean/curate/dedup, fix mixture (often the highest ROI per FLOP); (2) post-training — SFT on targeted weaknesses + preference/RLVR where verifiable (big gains, cheap vs pretraining); (3) architecture/efficiency — GQA/long-context/MoE only if that's the bottleneck. Run compute-matched single-change ablations; ladder winners up. Avoid "just train longer" unless the law says you're under-trained. State the decision rule for each bet.
Drill 8 — Critique: "We added component C and accuracy rose, so C causes the gain."▶
That's a correlation, not a causal, claim without an ablation. Adding C may have co-introduced more parameters, more tuning, a different data order, or a longer schedule. To support causality: (1) ablate — same setup, C removed, everything else fixed; gain should vanish. (2) dose-response — vary C's strength, expect monotonic effect. (3) mechanism — does the proposed mechanism predict where C helps (which examples)? (4) compute-matched + multi-seed. Without these, "C causes the gain" is unsupported.
Drill 9 — You have 10 promising ideas. How do you pick which to pursue?▶
Score each on (impact × probability × differentiation) / cost, and prefer ideas with a cheap decisive test. Concretely: (1) what would scaling solve for free? (drop those). (2) which has the highest information-per-compute early experiment? (3) which is differentiated vs what everyone else is doing? (4) which has a clear kill criterion so you fail fast? Sequence them: run the cheapest high-information tests first, kill aggressively, double down on survivors. Taste = a portfolio of fast falsifiable bets, not one big gamble.
Drill 10 — A reviewer asks "why does your method work?" and you're not fully sure. How do you answer?▶
Be calibrated and constructive, not hand-wavy. State the leading hypothesis and the evidence for it, the alternative hypotheses, and the experiment that would distinguish them: "Our working explanation is M, supported by ablation A and the fact that it helps most on examples of type T. An alternative is M′; the discriminating test is E, which we're running." Showing you know what you don't know and how to find out is higher-taste than a confident wrong story.
Drill 11 — Design an ablation to isolate the contribution of one component C.▶
Change one thing. Keep architecture, data, compute, schedule, and seeds fixed; produce a variant with C removed/replaced by the natural null (identity, random, or the prior method). Run multiple seeds; report mean ± variance. Strengthen with a dose-response sweep of C and a mechanism probe (does C help exactly where its story predicts?). Watch for interactions: if C only helps with D, do the 2×2 (±C, ±D). Compute-match every cell. The claim "C contributes X" is only as good as the cell that differs only in C.
Drill 12 — When do you trust a small-scale result to transfer to large scale?▶
When it lies on a smooth, fitted trend you can extrapolate (scaling-law-like), the mechanism isn't scale-specific, and it's been compute-matched at each small scale. Validate by predicting a held-out larger run from smaller ones and checking it lands. Distrust transfer when: the effect is near noise, depends on a fixed hyperparameter not re-tuned at scale, or interacts with things that change with scale (batch size, LR, data mix, emergence). Taste: "I trust trends, not single points, and I confirm by extrapolation before committing big compute."
Drill 13 — A result replicates on 1 seed. How many seeds, and why?▶
One seed tells you nothing about variance. Use enough seeds to estimate the standard error and compare to the effect size: if the per-seed std is $s$, the SE of the mean over $k$ seeds is $s/\sqrt{k}$, so to resolve an effect $\Delta$ you need $k\gtrsim (z\,s/\Delta)^2$ — small effects need many seeds. Practically 3–5 for a sanity signal, more if the gain is within a couple std. Report mean ± std (or CIs), and prefer effects that survive the worst seed. "A gain smaller than seed-to-seed variance isn't a result yet."
Drill 14 — Propose how to evaluate a model's "physical reasoning."▶
Define the construct first: physical reasoning = predicting outcomes consistent with physics (object permanence, support/containment, dynamics, affordances). Build a battery that can't be solved by language priors or a single frame: (1) controlled synthetic scenes with counterfactual pairs (same caption, different physics) so shortcuts fail; (2) video where the answer requires temporal dynamics (shuffle/ablate frames to prove time is used — Day 4 §2); (3) intervention questions ("what if the support is removed?"). Metrics beyond accuracy: consistency across rephrasings, calibration, and a failure taxonomy. Guard against contamination and "looks-right but wrong-mechanism." (Connects to the 3D / physical-intelligence theme in the resume-prep doc — the eval must reward grounded prediction, not plausible captions.)
